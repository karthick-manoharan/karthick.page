On Shortening the Adoption Rate for a Desired Mental State

May 2026

Who this is for

Someone wondering why the adoption of becoming better feels unreal and cyclical despite sustained attempts for change.

The familiar cycle

So you read a book about improving time management. You went ahead and installed a Pomodoro timer, a Kanban-style task manager, and a time-blocking calendar ritual. In those moments — and in the days that followed — the experience of gaining momentum and letting go of the undesired was truly felt.

Let the weeks and months pass by. There you are, wondering how the derailment even happened in the first place. Things started off quite well. It felt like onwards and upwards from there on. But let's accept it — there we are again, at square one.

The question of permanence

How do I make the transition permanent, not ephemeral?

The truth is — it is not a do-once and repeat-forever. Does that mean one has to fight forever with suboptimal time management, planning, and prioritising skills all along the course of one's life? The answer is not a binary yes or no. It calls for a graceful acceptance. But mere acceptance alone would not lift the trouble.

Adopting the nature, not just the method

The transition is not limited to embracing optimal methods — it is about adopting the very nature behind them. What attributes to the feeling of failure is not the measured outcome of efficiency, but the understanding of the very process of getting to know the improvement methods and believing each one to be an elixir that eliminates the ailment once and for all.

To state it plainly: you might have fallen for the trap of imagination — having internalised change, adoption methods described and prescribed by somebody else, in your own imagination, not from a lived experience.

Pause

The catalyst is this — you have to be the inventor, the discoverer of the solutions. Not a mere embrace of readymade ones.

Crafting your own solution

This does not imply avoiding wisdom from others. It all comes down to crafting your own. To begin — design your own solution. Let go of the fear of failure in adopting it. That fear is the very thing one should aim to release.

If you have read a book, watched a video, or listened to a podcast in the area of your desired improvement — getting to know is not the whole job. The effectiveness of listening is multiplied with reflection. Write an essay. Record a voice journal. Capture your agreements and disagreements with the philosophy — where it resonates, where it would certainly fail.

These reflections are drawn from your lived, practised experience of adoption methods and failed moments. The reflection surfaces the gaps in the method. The truth is calling to customise. Allow time to change thoughts — what once felt truthful may later feel incomplete. That is the calibration of the mind in evaluating the value of wisdom that worked for someone else.

A conversation, not a final solution

The effectiveness — the rate of return — of getting to know what worked for others amounts to little when there is no partnership in co-architecting the solution. It is a conversation, not a final answer. You cannot simply wear someone's shirt assuming it will magically fit right. In some cases, it is a matter of luck. But by adopting the mindset of discovering your own solution, you may sometimes arrive at existing ones — only now, that lived experience, that journey, is what makes it stick.

Questions to sit with

  1. If the self-help authors, or someone claiming to be productive — how do you think they arrived at such solutions?
  2. Why is there more than one solution for approaching a problem?
  3. Do you prefer to be told of the secret recipe?
  4. Do you think teaching someone the structure and mechanics would change them overnight?
